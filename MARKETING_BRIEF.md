# YourScore — Marketing Brief

**For:** Marketing lead  
**Purpose:** Full context to develop a go-to-market strategy  
**Last updated:** May 2026

---

## The One-Liner

**YourScore ranks your football IQ against your mates — across every game, all season long.**

---

## What It Is (Plain English)

You watch a lot of football. So do your friends. But there's no scoreboard for who actually knows more about it.

YourScore fixes that. You create a league — a permanent table for your group — and it tracks everyone's points across every match anyone plays in, any tournament, all year. When you watch a game, you open a room, share the code, and live questions fire on everyone's phones during the match. Faster correct answers score more. Streaks multiply your points. At the end of the season, there's a real leaderboard and a real argument to be had about who actually knows football.

That's the core. But users can also engage with YourScore outside of live matches through **Challenges** — season-review quizzes for each Premier League club, completed any time, at your own pace. No live match required.

**It is completely free. No app download needed.**

---

## The Problem It Solves

Football fans already have fierce opinions. They already debate, text, and group-chat during every match. There's no shortage of passion — just no way to keep score.

Fantasy football solves part of this: it gives you a table, a rival, something to obsess over. But FPL is about predictions made before a match. YourScore is about what you know *during* it — in the moment, on your phone, under pressure.

**The job to be done:** *"I want to prove I know more about football than my mates, in a way that actually keeps score."*

---

## The Product in Full

There are several distinct ways a person can use YourScore. Marketing should understand all of them — not just the headline use case.

### 1. Leagues
The primary product and the main acquisition hook. A league is a persistent table that tracks a group's points across every match they play in — any tournament, all season. Create one in 30 seconds. Invite via WhatsApp or link. Points, accuracy %, and streaks all tracked. One league can run for an entire season across the World Cup, Euros, Champions League, and more.

Key stat shown on the table: **Pound for Pound (P4P)** — accuracy percentage, not just raw points. This creates a second leaderboard argument inside every group: the person with the most points vs the person who's right more often.

### 2. Rooms
Rooms are match-specific. You create one per fixture, share a 6-character code, and it closes when the match ends. Rooms feed into your league — every point you score in a room counts toward your group's permanent table. Without a league, rooms are still playable as one-off games.

### 3. Challenges
Solo, async quizzes — no live match, no room needed. Currently: 20-question season-review quizzes for every Premier League club (2025/26). Users can complete these any time. A leaderboard shows how each player ranks on each quiz, but requires an account to appear on it.

Challenges are a way to stay engaged with YourScore between matches, and a lower-commitment entry point for new users who want to try the product before committing to a league.

## Who Uses It

### A. The League Setter-Upper
Starts a league, invites the group, sets the tone. Probably already runs a Fantasy league. The kind of person who organises things for their friend group. They are the acquisition target because they bring other people with them — but they are not the only user.

### B. The League Member
Gets invited by a friend. Joins the room during a match. May never create their own league or room. Acquired virally through the setter-upper — not a target for paid acquisition, but an important part of the ecosystem.

### C. The Solo Football Fan
Has no league yet but wants to test their knowledge. Finds YourScore through social media, search, or a Challenge. Uses Challenges as an entry point. May later create or join a league.

### D. The Joiner
Hears about a room during a specific match, joins to play once. Might convert to a regular user if the experience is good.

Marketing needs to serve A and C directly. B and D are acquired through the product itself.

## What Makes It Different

There is no direct competitor doing this combination of things.

| Product | What it does | Why YourScore is different |
|---|---|---|
| Fantasy Premier League | Pre-match predictions, season-long | YourScore is live, real-time knowledge during the match — not forecasting |
| Pub quiz apps (Kahoot, etc.) | General knowledge quizzes | Not football-specific, not live-match-synced, not built around your group |
| Sports betting | Wagering on outcomes | No money, no legal friction, open to everyone, pure knowledge |
| WhatsApp bets | Informal, in a group chat | YourScore structures those same conversations with rules and a proper table |

The gap is real. There is nothing that does live quiz + persistent group league + solo challenges, built specifically for football.

## Tournaments and Longevity

YourScore is **not a World Cup app**. It is built for football year-round:

- **FIFA World Cup 2026** — June 11 to July 19, 2026. The biggest event in world football. Hosted in the USA. Over 5 billion viewers.
- **UEFA Champions League** — runs September through May
- **UEFA Euros**
- **Premier League** — 38 gameweeks per season
- **International breaks**, domestic cups, and any other fixture supported in the match database

A league set up for the World Cup will already be in place for the Euros. Points stack. The group stays engaged. This is a year-round retention story, not a tournament novelty.

## How the Product Grows Itself

The product has a natural viral loop built into its mechanic:

```
User creates a league
    ↓
Shares invite code or link with their group (typically 5–15 people)
    ↓
Those people join and play
    ↓
Some of them create leagues for their own groups
    ↓
Repeat
```

Every league creator is a distribution node. The acquisition cost of participants inside a league is effectively zero — they are recruited by their friend.

Beyond leagues, Challenges create a separate organic loop: a good score gets shared, which drives new users to try the quiz.

The marketing job is to acquire enough league creators and solo players to seed the loops. Once a group has a league, they return for every match with no additional push needed.

## Channels — Ranked by Opportunity, with Reasoning

### 1. Short-form video (TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts)
**Why first:** Football content already has massive native audiences on these platforms. Question-reveal clips — "Would you have got this right?" — are content-native, require no explanation, and demonstrate the product in 10 seconds. A single well-timed clip during a big match moment can reach millions. This is the highest-reach, lowest-cost channel available.

What to make: question clips tied to real match moments, score reveals, "my group's leaderboard after 8 games" screenshots.

### 2. Football communities (Reddit, Discord, Twitter/X)
**Why second:** These audiences are self-selected high-intent football fans already discussing the same topics YourScore quizzes them on. Reddit's r/soccer, r/FantasyPL, and club-specific subs have millions of active members. Twitter/X football discourse is live and real-time — perfectly timed content during a match can spread widely.

What to make: Challenge quiz results shared as posts, debate-starting question threads, league table screenshots.

### 3. WhatsApp (organic and targeted)
**Why third:** The product is built around WhatsApp sharing — the share flow is native. The best marketing on WhatsApp is the product itself: when someone shares a room code or league invite into a group chat, that is a marketing moment. Organic WhatsApp sharing should be tracked and the in-product share experience should be as frictionless as possible.

### 4. Football podcasts and YouTube channels (sponsorship/creator partnerships)
**Why fourth:** Football podcast audiences are loyal, engaged, and exactly the demographic. A mid-size football creator with 100k subscribers doing a "play along with us" episode generates high-quality users who understand the product before they sign up. This is more efficient than broad advertising because the audience is pre-qualified.

### 5. Search (SEO and occasional paid)
**Why fifth:** Search intent for "football quiz", "Premier League quiz", "Champions League predictions" is real and growing. The Challenges section is natural SEO territory — club-specific quiz pages can rank for long-tail queries. Paid search makes sense during tournament peaks (World Cup, Euros) when intent spikes.

## Geographies — Ranked by Opportunity, with Reasoning

### 1. United Kingdom
**Why first:** The highest concentration of passionate football fans per capita of any English-speaking market. FPL culture means the audience is already familiar with quiz/prediction mechanics and league tables. WhatsApp is the dominant group communication tool. The Premier League is the dominant cultural reference. Start here. Get density. 

### 2. Nigeria and Ghana
**Why second:** Both countries have enormous football fanbases — the Premier League and Champions League have huge followings. WhatsApp is the primary social platform, making the product's sharing mechanic immediately familiar. English-language. Large youth demographics with smartphones. These markets are underserved by existing football apps, which tend to focus on UK/Europe.

### 3. USA
**Why third:** The World Cup 2026 is hosted there. American football interest is growing — MLS is expanding, the USMNT has a new generation of followers, and 2026 will be the largest sporting event ever held in the country. This is a genuine inflection point for the US football audience. It is a harder market to crack than the UK (football is still competing with other sports) but the timing advantage is unique and temporary.

### 4. South Africa, Kenya, and East Africa
**Why fourth:** English-speaking, large football fanbases (Premier League is extremely popular), mobile-first internet usage, WhatsApp dominant. Similar profile to Nigeria/Ghana but slightly later in the priority list due to lower smartphone penetration compared to Nigeria.

### 5. Brazil and Argentina
**Why fifth:** Fanatical football markets with massive World Cup engagement. However, Portuguese and Spanish are the primary languages, which creates a localisation barrier. These markets are worth monitoring but require translation investment before they become primary targets.

## Key Messages

### What to lead with
- **"Your football IQ. Ranked."** — the actual headline on the site. It works. Use it.
- **"Prove you know more about football than your mates"** — the emotional driver
- **"Start a league. Points stack all season."** — the retention story
- **"Free to play. No app needed."** — removes every objection

### What not to lead with
- Rooms (the mechanic, not the story — leagues are the story)
- The technology
- Any comparison to trivia apps — this is a football product
- Anything that sounds complicated to set up

### The World Cup angle
The World Cup is a good "why now" for timely content and campaigns but should not be framed as the product's entire reason to exist. The product works for any tournament. Campaigns tied to the World Cup should feel like "now is a great time to set up your league" rather than "this is a World Cup product."

## Open Questions for the Marketer

1. **What is the single target number for end of World Cup group stage?** Active leagues? Active users? Registered accounts? Define the metric — everything else flows from it.

2. **Is there a founder or face for this?** Authentic founder content on TikTok/Instagram converts better than brand content at this stage. Is there a story worth telling?

3. **Which creator partnerships are realistic?** A deal with 3–5 football TikTokers (100k–1M followers) before June 11 could be the single highest-ROI spend available. Who is the right profile? UK-based, younger audience, Premier League / Champions League focused.

4. **How do we measure the viral loop?** If the product grows itself through invites, we need to understand the referral coefficient — how many new users does each league creator bring in on average? This number should drive how much to spend acquiring creators vs participants.

5. **What does the Challenges SEO opportunity look like?** There are 20 club-specific quiz pages already live. How competitive are those search terms? Is there a content strategy to build on top of them?
